swinging

Dad liked that the Foss Park Golf Course was cheap, never overcrowded, and a ten-minute drive from home. To the uninformed, there was no shot of finding yourself there except by sheer chance. Once you turn off Green Bay Road, every street yields equally unassuming one-story houses, ones with blue or tan siding and close-cropped midwestern lawns. The entrance is less of an entrance and more of a neighborhood street which ends in a paved parking lot delimited by large rocks. No part of the surrounding shrubbery or suburban charm divulges the course’s existence. But Dad always seemed to get us there by instinct alone. Without fail, we’d reach a bend in the road, and I’d see the worn sign beckoning us in.

Foss Park made up for its perceived exclusivity by being public—or is it the other way around? It looks like any other ordinary golf course. To the right—bordering the parking lot—a fleet of golf carts stands at attention, enclosed behind a wooden fence. Two squat brick buildings, neither very large, sit on either side of the asphalt path leading into the course. They house the equipment shop and the Bar & Grill. Straight ahead along the path is the putting green. The driving range is to the right and up a modest hill. And, of course, beyond them both, eighteen holes.

That first summer, I was seven. About once a week, my older brother and I were whisked away in the pulsating Illinois heat, our shabby clubs sweating themselves sick. Dad would park and then hoist our bags out. I’d hear him whistling as he selected his own clubs for that day from the ragtag assortment he kept loose in his trunk. Something untangled in him. Then, his movements became languid as the three of us started towards the golf ball dispenser. Dad would slide a crumpled ten into the machine; I’d be mesmerized by the dull clink of the balls falling into their latticed basket. Of course, Dad would carry it, the plastic handle straining under the weight. As soon as we’d made our way up to the driving range, we would stake our claim on three adjacent squares of turf. Unsheathe our drivers from their covers. Dad would divide the balls between us, roughly scattering my portion at my feet. We’d be there for an hour or two, launching balls down into the range.

More often than not, we’d be the only ones there on a given afternoon. Unsurprisingly, my childlike efforts—and my brother’s—would sink and fall so terribly shy of the yard markers. A look over at Dad revealed his shots streaking comet-like, way out in the distance. I’d note the way his hands were placed on the shaft of his 4-wood, the bend of his knees, the smooth arc of his swing. I’d copy it all as best as my scrawny limbs could manage. My shots would go a little straighter, rise a little higher, if still not very far. Yet Dad would pump his fist with pride, eyes widening in generous shock. I’d never felt closer to him. I’d never felt more like him.

When there was nothing left to shoot, we’d take one last look at the horizon, then turn, walk back down the hill, and drive home. So went the ritual. Unless it was a Friday. Then, if we’d already stayed past dusk, Dad would veer towards the Clubhouse on our way back to the car. He’d pull out a twenty from his wallet and exchange it for battered fried cod in butcher paper. We’d crowd around the fish, the smell of it entrancing, my brother and I practically salivating. Dad would cut it up and the three of us would share. Oftentimes he’d lecture us on something (I don’t remember what).

When golf season was over and we were left to our own devices, my brother and I had our real fun. At home, sometimes we were secret agents, legitimized by our ToysRUs walkie-talkies and slinking manner. Other times, we’d ride our bikes (with no hands!) around the empty cul-de-sac one street over. Or create elaborate family dramas with our stuffed animals. Or screech and laugh amongst ourselves about nothing in particular. We were good kids, I like to think, if a bit energetic. Kept ourselves entertained.

But we were restless, and Dad wanted us to love what he loved, before we succumbed to that impatience and pity for one’s parents which comes with growing older. The next summer, Dad signed us up for a summer-long program. For fifty-five dollars each, my brother and I would be taught both golf fundamentals and the pillars of good character. We would aim to imitate the good breeding that the sport apparently required. The program consisted of a weekly half-hour lesson on courtesy or respect or something of that sort, followed by practice. Sitting in a half circle in the grass, all the kids would take notes with those miniature golf pencils. Then we’d go out on the course for about an hour.

Afterwards, apples would be waiting for us in a cooler full of ice. Shiny with cold, they bobbed and shimmered as if suspended by magic. They were the same red I found upon closing my eyes, right after looking directly into the sun. Except for those moments following the long grueling holes, I have never liked red apples. (They’ve always been Dad’s favorite.)

The golfing we did that summer at Foss Park all runs together, the grass and the trees and the holes marked by checkered flags. The benches seemed at rest, spaced out along the course— placed there for the poor schmucks like us who were made to walk. You learn to tune out the jangly click of the clubs that accompanies every step, the awkward weight of the golf bag slung against your back.

Sometimes, out there with the quiet, I thought I saw what Dad did: Sun-weary and drenched awake, I imagined him coming alive with awe as a shot soared perfectly straight over the hills. He’d hear the thwack of a well-placed chip and close his eyes in relief. Or it’d all be a disaster, and he’d have to play again. Over and over, a breath, a swing, a scribbled number. I tried to love it. Since I was supposed to. Without complaint, I wore these horrific multicolor plaid shorts and a polo I ironed myself. Practiced my swing. Learned to enjoy pressing my tee into the ground, syringe-like. Memorized the weight of a golf ball in hand.

I am older now and I haven’t played in years. Recently, Mom told me: The day I came home from the hospital was a warm afternoon in August, crisp and windless. My father made his newborn daughter’s crib dutifully, yet with some sort of apology in his eyes. He kissed us goodbye and left to play nine holes. The greens were calling, and he had no choice but to answer. She remembers that in the thick of his obsession, he’d play twice a week, sometimes more. And when he wasn’t playing, he’d be here, at home, but really, he was on the golf course.

Mom was incredibly patient but absolutely uninterested in golf. I imagine the whole thing took her quite by surprise. When her and Dad met, twenty-odd years ago, he had never set foot on a golf course. Such places were imaginary things to a man grown up amongst land that was used to grow cassava and raise goats. In Nigeria, things had both grit and purpose. As a boy, he would tie together sheets of plastic to make kites—or wad up fabric and wind string around to make a ball to kick around with the other village kids. Back home, there was none of this manicured quasi-civility that takes place on the golf course. No white men looking sidelong at you and driving past with their golf carts and expensive watches and privilege.

When Dad came to the U.S. and a friend got him hooked on golf, he played out of stubbornness. The first time, he couldn’t hit the ball. Stood there swinging and missing, his brow furrowed. The challenge of it was tempting. It was a good anger. He’d come down hard on the ball, pummeling it and shanking it to the left, always left.

As a kid, I thought this all much simpler. All I knew was that Dad would drag us to pawn shops on the weekends to look at clubs. He’d find coupons online for a cheap tee time, call one of his friends on the drive home from work to trash-talk them into playing an unplanned nine holes. He’d disappear into the obsession. If the weather was nice enough, there would be a note on the kitchen table when I got home from school. “I couldn’t resist,” it would say. And he couldn’t. Golf took him over, brought out the extremes in him. It was as if he was a magnet drawn to the course. Often, he’d perform kitchen re-enactments of games he’d won. His arms would gesture and swing wildly, his voice rising as he recounted a triumph. He’d remember the score hole by hole, chart the path of each hit with his hands as he grinned. When recounting a loss, he’d shake his head. He’d watch the professionals on TV and bemoan their failures. Practice his swing in the bathroom, hands fisted. “This stupid game,” he would say. Even when he broke clubs in frustration, he had to keep going back. Because there were the other games, the ones where every hit was perfect and you had no idea why.

Dad had surgery for sciatica a couple years ago and his back hasn’t been the same since. When it got warm again, he bought a right-handed glove, taught himself to play lefty. It didn’t really take. Last year was the first time in ages that he didn’t play at all. Not once the whole summer did he come back sweaty, smelling of grass with a gleam in his eyes. There were no hours of not knowing where he was and realizing there’s only one place he could be—out with the unending grass, squinting into the distant sun.
