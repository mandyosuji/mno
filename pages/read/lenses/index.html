lenses

It comes into view, the blurry, smug hot air balloon hovering somewhere above the road. I only know it’s a hot air balloon because she’s told me so, the assistant with the soft smile and the pitying eyes. All I can make out for myself is a fuzzy, bright-red daub positioned roughly center. My old nemesis. It floats above the presumed grass—a muddled pea green swath on either end of the gray strip leading off into the distance. Focus on the balloon, the assistant says. I don’t know her name, the plastic clip-on nametag too far away for me to make out.

I look at the picture through the lens like she tells me. Fidget with the hands in my lap. The road and the field and the balloon remain out of focus. If there are clouds in the sky, to me they blend into the rest of its blue. For all I know, there could be far-off birds whirling above cows that dot the surrounding fields. I must assume, must gather what little evidence I can. It would be nice to know for sure.

The assistant is scribbling numbers down onto her clipboard. A sense of scrutiny and the overhead florescent lights remind me too much of specimens cut open on the lab table. Last year at school we dissected earthworms—peeled their skin back, pinned it down, catalogued their insides. They didn’t squirm because they were already dead. My chin starts to quiver against the plastic lip of the machine, some clunky white sterilized unpleasant thing. I can’t overcome the urge to squint and pull away. The assistant furrows her brow, her smile gone.

She says something reassuring. Then tells me to put my face back into the contraption, that it won’t be much longer. Soon a puff of air will dart into each of my reluctant eyes—the only ones I have. They say it measures the pressure. I cautiously nestle my face back where it should be, smell the sting of rubbing alcohol. I’m unsure whether the tears begin because of the impending discomfort of the puff of air, or simply a pathetic awareness of myself. Either way, this is the moment I abandon all dignity. Suddenly my breaths come up short. I blink and blink to no avail. Wetness gathers in beads on my eyelashes. Then the tears spill over down onto my cheeks.

I sit back, collect myself. Just a creature wet-eyed, blinded, small. Aside from the assistant standing awkwardly behind the equipment, my mother is in the room, even though I am too old for that and have done this all before. She leans over and squeezes my hand. I silently wipe the tears away, avoiding eye contact. Square my shoulders and sit forward with my eyes open, accepting. I flinch, then still. The puff of air is over quickly.

I am eleven, or maybe twelve, it doesn’t matter. I’ve lost track, from my later vantage. It can feel as if every time I’ve been in this room is faintly visible at once, like overlaid sheets of tracing paper. (Though this memory we’re in now was the first and only time I’d ever cried mid-examination.)

It happens annually, my prescribed once-a-year torment. My eye exam is at the Lenscrafters inside the mall. It’s directly across from the Rainforest Café, outside of which a plastic snake flicks its tongue robotically while coiled round a plastic vine-covered tree. From outside you can see the tanks of real live fish and the faux-happy hostesses. Every so often, the tacky fog machine in the corner pumps out more illusion. Some lights flash, meant to be lightning. A myriad of animatronic animals trumpet and roar as the simulated thunderstorm passes through. The scene looks almost believable, if you squint and have the raw faith of a five-year-old. In any case, it makes this Lenscrafters seem like the wrong place to undergo serious examinations of one’s optical well-being. The whole thing would be laughable, if I were not the one in the padded chair getting the insides of my eyes blown on and judged.

Having survived the whole tearing-up debacle, we move to the next stage of humiliation. I am now in the room with various lenses stacked neatly on the side table. Here we have the man who will ordain my fate: the optometrist. He, at least, has the decency to look grave and faintly apologetic. As if he, too, knows that all this prodding is too much. That I’d prefer my eyes be left alone. Regardless, he is obligated to proceed through all the motions. On his computer, he pulls up the large-scale photos of my retinas, which neither of us want to look at. Says everything seems normal, then clicks out of the window. He shines a bright light directly in my eyes which almost makes me hiss, checks if I have become color blind since last year, witnesses my being made a fool by that chart with all the letters and blobs. I sweat a little each time he asks me to pick between option one or option two. He waits as I pause and painstakingly compare them. Sometimes I make him go back and forth more than once so I can be sure. It’s all rather embarrassing.

I can’t help but forgive the optometrist. He doesn’t know that these days, every night before I go to bed, I look at myself in the mirror mounted on the back of my bedroom door. I stare directly into my own pupils for some minutes, then I blink and turn off the light. Eyes wide open, I lie there on my bed and think about going blind in my sleep. Convince myself that I can’t see the sliver of moonlight coming through the blinds. Every now and then I get back up, turn on the light, look for a moment longer. Check to make sure that I still look how I do in my own memory. It’s easy to pretend that the worst thing that can happen would be losing my sight. Pretend that is the biggest unending darkness. (The one coming for us all.) At least I can prepare myself.

I’m not sure when this strange routine started, or the fear. It wasn’t always this way. My first pair of glasses was in the second grade. I was unconcerned, naïve, not yet exposed to the true life of the myopic. The leering fingers held up, mocking. How many? Triumphant and wicked glee from those who just can’t believe someone’s sight could be so bad. (OD) -6.00, (OS) -4.50, if you must know.

Seven-year-old me used to imagine that someday I would have perfect vision and listen to podcasts and muse on far-off objects. That if I ate enough carrots and looked long distances in the sun, this smudgy period could be but a blip in my visual record. But the optometrist checks his notes, pronounces me more sightless than last year. I sigh. I knew it was coming, having read by lamplight and stayed indoors since I saw him last.

I suppose I’d pre-mourned this prognosis, what with my stint back there in the other room. But by now I am used to the news of worsening. Perhaps I have brought it on myself. I can’t look at anyone putting in contacts, and something inside me whimpers at that scene in Alice and Wonderland (2010) when the Bandersnatch gets an eye yanked out. Via a cruel logic, it makes sense that I am doomed to dwell often on what I least like to think about. Every time I struggle to make out a friend’s face from many paces away. Or strain to read small text. Or rub my eyes inadvertently beneath my thick-lensed glasses. My mind gets stuck thinking about my sclera and cornea and irises. The limbal ring, pupil, retina. All the other parts I can’t call by name. It’s all so fragile, two orbs fleshy and exposed.

None of us enjoys going to any sort of physician, surely. But at the optometrist’s, the whole affair feels truly intimate. It’s not the same as hitting my knee with a hammer or measuring how tall I am or even taking X-rays of my teeth. Somehow, it’s all so different. I don’t think about my knees or limbs or teeth with the same daunting reverence. For whatever reason, I never worry that any of them will suddenly stop working. Only that the dark, quiet sheet of night will become all there is. It’s a hypnotic fear. One that lingers reliably within arm’s reach. Comforting in its sameness.

So again, I am thinking of the image they show at the beginning of each trip to the optometrist. The one with the hot air balloon smack-dab in the center. The other day, I performed a radical act of exposure therapy. I looked up the image online. It had never once occurred to me to do this. I’d never thought to see for myself the scene which haunts me still, reminds me that there is more to be noticed than I can notice. But alone, with my glasses on, I blew it up to about 500 times the regular size, took it all in. It turns out there’s a car about halfway down the road that I’ve never seen before. The hot air balloon is other colors besides red. Whisps of clouds surround it. It’s sort of beautiful, now that I see it. I memorize the textures, the sharpness. All so clear.
