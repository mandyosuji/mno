Montell Fish Changed on Me

FIRST DISCOVERY

No one wants to be preached at. So Montell Fish sang in muddled falsetto. His lyrics were raw, almost painfully earnest. When I found him, they were all about God.

If I could write You a song, Lord, I wouldn't hold nothing back
I would tell You my all; to my sin, I've been running back
And I can't hold it all, burden is too heavy on my soul
Lord, please come cleanse me, come make me reign whole
'Cause I need Your—, feeling like I'm in Job
Feeling like I've been losing touch with my soul
And I don't know where to go
Father, bring me to the light, I can't find my way back home
I need You now

I remember the moment well enough, even though it was eight years ago. My older sister was home, probably for Christmas. We were sitting at the table in our living room, which is mahogany and, in my memory, Last Supper-length. She gently pressed her earbuds into my ears, pressed play without preamble. Ever the paradigm of a youngest sister, I received the offering without resistance. Through the earbuds came a voice that was decidedly angelic, yet simultaneously guttural. True.

Montell rumbled the opening lines of the album I would listen to time and time again. The beginning verse ricocheted between left and right ear. I pushed the earbuds in further, intrigued. Stayed there for the next 51 minutes as I heard someone be more honest before God than I knew was possible.

He was not the man on the corner with a megaphone, spewing condemnation at passerby while an audience pretended to ignore him. He was not the hunched-over Asian lady who lies in wait at an intersection, shakily forcing a flyer into your palms, or who walks several uncomfortably close paces next to you as she pries at your belief in Jesus. Montell was fervent in a different way.

REBIRTH

In earlier years, my parents orbited religion as something heavy yet negotiable. The way I remember it, they realized they had in common a faint attachment to Catholicism, shrugged, and tried to haul us all to church more Sundays than not. So, faith was something gauzy and far-off. I kept it at arm’s length. We all did.

In my mind’s eye, I see the stone font of holy water stationed at the entrance of St. Paul the Apostle Catholic Church. I dip my fingers halfheartedly into the forgiving wet. My knees dimple the cushioned kneeler at noontime mass, all the way in the back where God cannot see me. I do not know what Lent is, or why the priest is dressed like that, what it means for a name to be hallowed. I do the sign of the cross over and over and feel nothing.

But here, in our living room with my elbow propped on the mahogany, I encounter something new. A veil torn in two, confession with no holds barred. Listening to Montell feels like sitting with him as he sits with God. Every song feels like a prayer, one I didn’t know I could say.

Sometimes I feel like Peter
Denied You and I cannot deny that I did
Can I rest my head on Your shoulders again?

Suddenly, there was no hiding from God. Montell drags you in up close, zooms in to blood freshly drawn. When Montell sang about Peter, listener and vocalist alike seemed to collapse into one single denier-of-Christ. The lyrics hit my chest—a confession of guilt that stripped me bare. Yet this understanding felt like release, a breath exhaled. Permission, of a sort, to be imperfect before God.

At my church now, Pastor wears his Nikes onstage, and his sermon notes are riddled with typos. I think this is who God wants us to be. Not the super-Christians who blankly smile and perform belief. Not the buttoned-up amens in harmony. Not the people who plead faultlessness and cast judgement from their blinding cloud. The plank in your own eye, etcetera. Let’s stop pretending. Sometimes I think that’s what Montell is trying to say.

There is an undeniable desperate clamoring in the way that he sings. This is a man in need. You can almost hear the muffling of bedroom walls, the atmosphere of keening secrecy. Every word is between him and God, you’re just listening in.

I don't know what else, to say
If You're not here with me
Then I'm surely empty
Father, I need You right now
I know that You're all around
But I just need to hear the sound
Of Your voice, if I can

This is not the tired bore of CCM where we all nod our heads thoughtfully and unfurl our palms at all the right moments—to what, play holy? Who do we think we’re fooling? No, this is real desire. More real than the vaguely inspirational pop where Jesus gets treated in the same vein as cheesy Hallmark cards. Montell’s songs tilt on the axis of Psalm-like cries for more. He says it all in that wilting voice and you cannot help but listen.

PERSONA

The first sign was the shaved head. A few years after my first encounter with his music, Montell deleted everything from his Instagram page and posted a photo of himself shorn, blurry. At first, I wasn’t sure it was him.

But there he was, the smooth planes of his face. The dreads were gone, but the flat sadness of his brown eyes was the same. Montell Fish remained the name I used for him, but it was never his given name, Montell Frazier. At the same time, he was Lord’s Child, then ghostboi, then dj gummy bear. A conveyor belt of identities to slip into and out of at will.

Montell exists in chorus with himself. On his songs, you can often siphon out a ghostly doubled vocal track settled just behind the melody. Or one of his aliases will release a song that lists another as featured. This all, for some reason, disturbs me. That someone can be two different people at once. What’s worse is that his music takes roughly the same shape, regardless of what name he writes it under. Almost as if he relishes the continuous introduction, the slight moment of unrecognizability. It’s trippy, makes me feel off-balance. The whole thing reminds me of nesting dolls, if they were identical. He is a man inside a man inside a man.

Still, his music has a consistent smoky quality, a lofi haze pulsing and whispered. That first album I listened to was released under the Lord’s Child moniker. It was centered around coming back to God after time spent dormant, and every song rings with overt praise. So too, did all the songs released as Montell Fish around that time, such as “Holy God” and “On my Way.”

Hence why the first reinvention came as such a shock. Suddenly Montell seemed to compartmentalize his faith into something decidedly in the past. Overnight, his online presence devolved into cryptic captions and shadowy images and a glaring lack of God-centered songs. He was something new.

The truly dedicated fans somehow pieced together that he had gone through a breakup, with a girl named Jamie, which was the name of his new album. I guess he went through two. One with her, and one with God.
